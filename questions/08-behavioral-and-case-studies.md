# 8. Behavioral & Case Studies

Cross-disciplinary collaboration and real-world engineering tradeoffs — carbon capture bio-architecture spans biology, process engineering, and rigorous measurement/accounting all at once.

---

### Q: Tell me about a time you had to collaborate with someone from a very different technical background (e.g., a biologist working with an MRV/carbon accounting specialist, or a process engineer working with a synthetic biologist) on a carbon capture project. 🟡

**What a strong answer includes:**
- A specific example showing genuine two-way collaboration — the biological engineering perspective and the measurement/accounting or process engineering perspective each meaningfully shaping the project's design, rather than the MRV or accounting work happening as an afterthought once the biology was already finalized.
- Evidence of proactively building shared vocabulary across disciplines, particularly given how much specialized terminology exists on both the biology side and the carbon accounting/MRV side.
- A concrete example showing how this collaboration led to a better outcome (e.g., an MRV specialist's input on additionality requirements meaningfully changing the experimental/field design from the start, rather than the project structure making rigorous additionality demonstration difficult after the fact).

**Follow-ups:**
- What specific practices helped ensure MRV and measurement considerations were integrated into technical design decisions from the start, rather than being treated as a downstream reporting concern?

---

### Q: Describe a time you had to critically evaluate whether a promising carbon capture result (yours or someone else's) actually represented genuine, durable carbon removal, versus a less climate-significant outcome that could have been mistaken for one. 🟡

**What a strong answer includes:**
- A specific example connecting to the capture-versus-storage distinction discussed in section 1, or the additionality concept discussed in section 5 — demonstrating genuine, applied technical rigor in distinguishing an impressive-sounding capture result from a genuinely durable, additional carbon removal claim.
- Evidence of appropriately skeptical, careful evaluation even when the more impressive-sounding interpretation would have been more exciting to report or more favorable for the project/team.
- An honest account of how this evaluation was communicated to relevant stakeholders, and the outcome.

**Follow-ups:**
- How do you personally maintain this kind of rigorous skepticism about your own team's or your own results, given the natural incentive (and, in this field, real financial incentive) to interpret a promising result in the most favorable possible light?

### Q: Walk me through how you'd approach a new project brief: "evaluate whether an enhanced biological carbon fixation approach could be a viable, credit-generating carbon removal project for a specific site or context." 🟡

**Case-study structure — a strong candidate would:**
1. **Clarify the full intended pathway from capture to durable storage** (section 1) before evaluating any specific biological engineering approach — what happens to the fixed carbon after capture, and how durable is that storage pathway genuinely expected to be.
2. **Assess the site/context's genuine suitability** for the proposed approach — land/resource availability, climate/environmental conditions, and realistic techno-economic viability (section 6) given the site's specific characteristics, rather than assuming a generically promising technology will work well anywhere.
3. **Design the project with MRV and additionality requirements integrated from the start** (section 5) — establishing appropriate baseline measurement and controlled comparison design before any intervention begins, rather than retrofitting measurement rigor after the fact.
4. **Evaluate whether biological engineering is genuinely necessary or advantageous** for this specific application compared to a simpler, non-engineered biological approach or a purely mechanical/chemical alternative (sections 2, 3, 6), applying the "is the added complexity justified" evaluation discipline emphasized throughout this repo.
5. **Plan a staged validation and scale-up pathway** (section 6) with realistic timeline and cost expectations, and early engagement with relevant regulatory and carbon-methodology requirements (section 7) rather than treating these as late-stage considerations.
6. **Communicate honest, uncertainty-explicit expectations** to the stakeholder about likely net carbon removal, cost, and timeline, consistent with the technology-readiness honesty emphasized throughout this repo.

**Follow-ups:**
- The stakeholder is primarily interested in the project for its public relations/marketing value and seems less focused on rigorous MRV than you believe is warranted. How would you handle this?

### Q: Tell me about a time you had to communicate honestly to a stakeholder (a client, investor, or the public) that a carbon capture project's actual net removal or permanence was more limited or uncertain than initial, more optimistic framing had suggested. 🟡

**What a strong answer includes:**
- A specific example of honestly conveying a genuine gap between an optimistic initial framing (whether the candidate's own earlier framing, or one set by others on the team) and a more rigorous, MRV-grounded assessment, without either minimizing the correction's importance or being unnecessarily alarmist.
- Evidence of framing the correction constructively — proposing a revised, more defensible claim and a path toward strengthening the project's actual performance or measurement rigor, rather than simply delivering bad news.
- A concrete, positive outcome showing the correction led to a more credible, ultimately more valuable project or claim.

**Follow-ups:**
- How do you personally handle organizational, investor, or market pressure to make more aggressive carbon removal claims than your own technical assessment supports, while maintaining scientific and professional integrity?

---

### Q: Describe a situation where you had to decide between pursuing a more scientifically ambitious carbon-fixation enhancement (e.g., novel pathway engineering) versus a more established, lower-risk approach, given project timeline, cost, or credibility considerations. 🔴

**What a strong answer includes:**
- A clear, specific articulation of the actual tradeoff considered — the ambitious approach's potential upside in fixation performance or cost, weighed honestly against its technical risk, longer validation timeline, and the importance of early project credibility in a field where MRV rigor and demonstrated reliability matter enormously for stakeholder trust (section 5).
- Evidence of a structured, staged approach to de-risking the ambitious direction (e.g., computational screening or small-scale pilot validation, per sections 2 and 4) before committing significant resources.
- An honest account of the outcome and reflection on whether the decision was the right call in hindsight.

**Follow-ups:**
- How do you personally weigh the reputational and credibility risk of an ambitious carbon removal claim underperforming publicly, against the potential climate and business value of pursuing a more ambitious technical approach?
